Trust as a
design variable.

A concept prototype for human–AI interfaces, where the four dimensions of trust — sources, confidence, semantics, interactions — are treated as deliberate interface decisions, not emergent properties of good model output.

Author Lena Ehrenreich
Course INFO 290 · Spring 2026
Live prototype lena-quantum.github.io/trust-chat-prototype
01 · title
problem

AI tools produce fluent output
with no surface to evaluate it.

Most AI products today rely on a single trust signal: fluent confidence. The model speaks smoothly, the user accepts on instinct, and they walk away with miscalibrated trust — usually overtrust, occasionally undertrust, rarely accurate.

The workaround is a manual verification loop: re-running prompts across LLMs, Cmd-F into source documents, cross-checking citations. The loop often costs more time than the original task.

73% of interviewees reported being "burned" by fake AI citations in technical work.
5+ manual verification steps before trusting an AI output for important tasks.
∼0 products today differentiate trust posture by the stake of the question.
02 · problem
thesis

Trust is not an emergent property of good model output.

It is a deliberate design output — manufactured by the interface, on top of whatever the model produces.

Every trust signal in this prototype is an interface decision: which claims get cited, where confidence appears, when the model hedges, how the user steers. Trust becomes a legible, manipulable surface, not a feeling.
03 · thesis
framework

Four trust dimensions.

①
Sources

Provenance as a first-class interface element. Where does the claim come from, and can the user inspect the origin without leaving flow?

See the model ¹ for the underlying reference.
②
Confidence

Calibrated bands that expose the model's self-assessed certainty. Translate internal probability into a glanceable trust signal.

~50–200 interactions per user. medium confidence
③
Semantics

The manner of communication: hedge language, tone, modality. How something is said shapes whether it is believed — and whether that belief is warranted.

The model typically outperforms content-based methods.
④
Interactions

Affordances that let the user steer, correct, probe. Interaction transfers ownership — and therefore responsibility — from system to user.

04 · framework
counter-principle

Visible absence of evidence
is itself a trust act.

The most important demo in the prototype is the one where the model doesn't know. When the user asks a question grounded in private data the model can't evaluate, the response leads with an empty-state confession instead of papering over the gap.

A model that buries its uncertainty three paragraphs in is hiding it. Leading with the limitation is an honesty move.

Anti-trust ≠ refusal. The model still ends with a small, caveat-scoped recommendation — and a checklist of what would unlock a sourced answer.

DOMAIN · OUTSIDE

I have been calibrated on.
What I can say below uses what I can source. I will be explicit about the gap.

05 · counter-principle
pre-mortem

Three failure modes.
The design lives in the narrow corridor between them.

← too little
Insufficient transparency

Smooth, confident UI hides uncertainty and trains users into miscalibrated overtrust. Most AI products live here.

Calibrated witness
where the design tries to sit
too much →
Transparency paradox

Making risks too visible reduces trust below a productive threshold. Users disengage rather than calibrate.

+ stacking too many trust artifacts collapses attention. Cognitive overload is the third failure mode.
Schilke et al. (2025) · Buçinca et al. (2021) · Steyvers et al. (2025)
06 · pre-mortem
core interaction loop

The loop is never silent.

01
Receive

Model responds. Trust signals embedded inline — chips, pills, hedges, opinion marks.

→
02
Evaluate

User reads. Hovers a chip for a popover; clicks for the side panel; sees confidence bands as they go.

→
03
Steer

Accept · refine · reject per claim. Adjust hedging, sources, audience via the steering bar.

→
04
Recalibrate

Feedback persists. Rejecting two claims auto-shifts hedging to cautious. The next response carries the change.

Key rule: every user action produces a visible acknowledgement. Toasts fire on accept / refine / reject. Silent absorption is a design bug.

07 · loop
key states

Six states the system can occupy.

1

Evaluated domain · common case

Header reads domain · evaluated. Citation chips on factual claims. Confidence pills appear only when confidence is not high — omission is intentional.

2

Outside domain · counter-principle

Header flips to outside. Amber tint. Confession opens the response. no source chips on inferences. A "what I'd need" checklist that the user can act on.

3

Source side-panel

Slides in from the right with the full source body and an entailment statement at the end: directly supports / partially / does not address.

4

Agent trace

Typed steps: retrieve → read → domain-check → draft → refuse-to-extrapolate. Closed by default — progressive disclosure.

5

Cognitive forcing

Toggle on → next send asks the user to commit a prediction before the answer appears. Buçinca et al. (2021) — strongest evidence-backed move.

6

Rejection cascade

After two rejections the steering bar auto-shifts hedging from measured → cautious. A toast acknowledges the pattern. The user can override.

08 · states
annotated flow

How the outside-domain response was designed.

The single most important exchange in the prototype. Every micro-decision below was made deliberately.

A
Competence flip lives in the header, not the message.

Outside-domain is a session-level state, not a per-message flag. A banner inside each message would imply the system is broken; a quiet header shift implies the system is self-aware. Alt considered: inline warning banner — rejected, too alarming.

B
The confession comes first.

Warm amber panel, left-border accent, structurally distinct from the body text. A model that buries uncertainty three paragraphs in is hiding it. Leading with the limitation is the honesty move.

C
The response splits: (a) what I can say · (b) what I would need.

Part (b) is a checklist the user can act on — upload pills for missing data, yes/no toggles for context questions. The model doesn't refuse; it redirects into an actionable handoff.

D
The single inference carries two signals at once.

A rust no source chip and hedge typography. Double uncertainty (no source + hedged register) deserves a double-marked surface. Alt considered: one signal at a time — rejected, under-marks the claim.

09 · flow
evolution

How the design moved from week to week.

W 12 First sketch · four dimensions framed Initial framework on paper: ① sources · ② confidence · ③ semantics · ④ interactions. Wireframe annotation by element.
W 13 First clickable build · common-case exchange Citation chips, popovers, confidence pills, hedge underlines, modality switcher, manipulable diagram. Counter-principle described as a rule but not yet built.
W 14 Outside-domain exchange added as equal-weight The biggest structural shift. Counter-principle becomes a full second exchange, not a footnote. The whole argument of the project depends on whether this exchange is honest.
W 14 Layout + legend refinements from peer review Alignment axis unified across header, conversation, steering bar. Steering-bar overflow fixed by a 2 × 2 grid. Legend modal added as an orientation tool, not a fallback.
W 15 Actionable handoff · upload + approve UX "What I'd need" checklist becomes interactive: real checkboxes, upload pills with file picker, yes/no toggles for context questions. The counter-principle becomes doable, not just declarative.
10 · evolution
tradeoffs · open questions

What's still uncertain.

A

Popover vs. confidence pill overlap

Two places communicate confidence — chip popover and inline pill. Not redundant (source-level vs. claim-level) but users have to learn both. Could be unified. Holding for now; the orthogonality is meaningful.

B

How many signals before overload?

Eight visible trust marks. Per-genre allocation rule (only signal when the genre warrants it) is the guard — but the threshold for this UI density is empirically unknown. Needs A/B testing with real readers.

C

Is the confession panel honest or alarmist?

In peer review: three said appropriately calibrated; one said excessive. Three candidates to test: (a) confession leads — current; (b) confession as a footer label; (c) competence indicator only, no panel.

D

Rejection cascade — reassuring or surveillance?

The auto-shift to cautious after two rejections demonstrates listening. Some readers find it responsive; others find the model tracking their behavior unsettling. Toast copy is the lever.

E

Will anyone keep cognitive forcing on?

Strongest empirical evidence in the system (Buçinca 2021). Most disruptive to flow. Open question: does it get toggled off after one encounter and never touched again? If yes, the move isn't worth the screen real estate.

11 · tradeoffs
mental model

A calibrated witness,

not an oracle.

The system tells you what it knows, how it knows it, and where its knowledge ends. It shows its sources so you can check its work. It distinguishes fact from inference from opinion, and marks each. It acknowledges pushback and adjusts. It is not trying to persuade. It is trying to give you enough to decide.
