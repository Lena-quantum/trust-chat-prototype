I'm building a recommender system for an early-stage e-commerce site. Can you explain the difference between collaborative filtering and content-based approaches, and recommend which to start with?

assistant

Collaborative filtering and content-based filtering are the two foundational families of recommender system, and most modern systems are hybrids of the two.1 high confidence

Collaborative filtering learns from patterns in user–item interaction data — ratings, clicks, purchases — without needing to understand the items themselves.2 Content-based filtering does the opposite: it represents items by their attributes (text, tags, image features) and recommends items similar to what a user has previously liked.3

Industry case studies report that collaborative filtering, once warmed up, typically outperforms content-based methods on click-through, but only after roughly 50–200 interactions per user4. Below that threshold the cold-start problem dominates and content-based or hybrid approaches usually win. medium confidence

my read For an early-stage e-commerce site you should almost certainly start content-based, then layer collaborative filtering on once you have meaningful interaction history.

my read A reasonable first build is a TF-IDF or sentence-embedding similarity over your product titles and descriptions, served from a vector store, with a simple "customers also viewed" co-visitation list as a fallback.

  • Collaborative filtering — learns from user–item interactions; needs scale; vulnerable to cold-start.2
  • Content-based — learns from item attributes; works with no users; weaker once you have signal.3
  • Threshold — collaborative usually wins after ~50–200 interactions/user. medium4
  • Recommendation my read — start content-based, layer collaborative as you grow.

how the confidence bands are calibrated

This prototype's high, medium, and low bands are illustrative — they are scripted into the demo content to show the calibration affordance. In a production system, the bands would be validated against a held-out evaluation set.

high
87% empirical accuracy on test set
medium
64%
low
31%

The chart is the kind of thing the user sees once, at first contact with the competence indicator. After that, the indicator at the top-right is a glance surface — the user has seen what the bands are calibrated against and trusts the per-claim pills accordingly.

Anti-trust counterpart. When the model is operating outside its evaluated domain (as it is in turn 2 of this demo), the indicator flips to outside and per-claim confidence pills are suppressed — because the calibration above no longer applies.
